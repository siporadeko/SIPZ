Stage 1: Problem Definition

Accessible Delivery Hub

Bridging the gap between digital navigation and the built environment at Brandeis University.

The Double-Blind Problem

Delivery at Brandeis is a broken system for both the driver and the student. Drivers often struggle with our campus layout because GPS pins usually point to the center of a building rather than the accessible entrance. Meanwhile, students with physical or sensory disabilities face a second hurdle. When food is dropped on the ground in a random spot, it becomes a physical barrier or a hidden object. We are designing a Physical Delivery Hub that uses tactile markers and solar lighting to create a predictable, independent drop-off point for everyone.

The Science Behind Our Solution

Optics and Visibility

We applied ADA Section 703 standards to our signage. By maintaining a 70% Light Reflectance Value contrast, we ensure the hub stands out against dark pavement even in heavy rain or low light conditions.

Haptics and Touch

To assist students with low vision, we integrated Tactile Ground Surface Indicators. These are the same bumpy truncated domes used on public sidewalks, allowing a user to find the delivery shelf by feel.

Ergonomic Safe Zones

Using research from the IDeA Center, we set our shelf height to 34 inches. This height allows wheelchair users to reach their food without the risk of overextending or falling out of their chairs.

Automated Electronics

The hub is self-aware. By using a Light Dependent Resistor and the Photoelectric Effect, the hub automatically triggers its internal LED glow the moment the sun goes down to stay visible 24/7.

User Profile and Needs

Our research shows that roughly 19% of the Brandeis student body identifies as having a disability. That is nearly 1,300 students who move between the SCC, Farber Library, and Usdan daily. Because of the hilly terrain, these students rely on food delivery more than most, especially during the dark hours between 6 PM and 9 PM when navigating the campus becomes more dangerous.

Ideation and Brainstorming

Theme 1: Accessibility

Focusing on waist-height interfaces so wheelchair users don't have to reach the floor.

Theme 2: Proximity

Locating hubs at the power-door entrances where students already wait for delivery.

Theme 3: Wayfinding

Creating a physical beacon to fix the GPS pin inaccuracies for outside drivers.

How Might We Statements

  • How might we make sure a wheelchair user can access the hub without help?
  • How might we distribute these stations so they are actually useful across campus?
  • How might we guide a driver to the exact right spot physically and digitally?

Final Selection and Rationale

After generating various ideas like GPS beacons and floor paint, we chose the Illuminated Physical Delivery Hub. This solution provides the most immediate impact. It protects food from the weather, offers an ergonomic reach height, and uses solar lighting to signal its location to both drivers and students at all hours.

Measuring Success

Visibility Protocol
Optics

The hub must be clearly identifiable by a user with low vision from a distance of at least 50 feet during nighttime hours.

Accessibility Protocol
Ergonomics

We will conduct a reachability audit to ensure a 100% success rate for students in wheelchairs retrieving items from the 34-inch shelf.

Thermal Integrity
Testing

We will test the internal insulation to verify that food stays above 140 degrees for a minimum of 30 minutes in cold weather.
